# UX Research Methods Reference
Version: 2.1 | Updated: 2026-07-11

## Choose method by decision
- Discover unmet needs: contextual inquiry, interviews, diary study
- Understand language and mental models: interviews, open card sorting
- Validate information architecture: closed card sort, tree test
- Validate task completion: moderated or unmoderated usability test
- Test first impression/comprehension: five-second test
- Measure prevalence or satisfaction: survey with appropriate sampling
- Compare alternatives: controlled preference test or experiment
- Diagnose live behavior: analytics, funnels, session evidence, support logs
- Validate accessibility: expert review plus users of relevant assistive technologies

No method proves everything. State what the selected method can and cannot establish.

## Research brief
Define decision, primary question, current evidence, target behavior, participant criteria, method, sample rationale, ethics, timeline, analysis, limitations, and success criteria.

## Participant selection
Recruit based on behavior and context required by the research question. Include appropriate range of experience, device, language, accessibility needs, and environment. Do not use demographic stereotypes as substitutes for behavior.

Qualitative sample size depends on segment diversity, study scope, and saturation. Quantitative sample size requires statistical planning. Five users can reveal useful issues but do not establish prevalence.

## Usability tasks
Use realistic scenarios without naming the target control. Define success, partial success, failure, critical error, assistance, expected path, alternate valid path, and stopping condition before testing.

## Metrics
Record unassisted success, assisted success, failure, errors, time on task, confidence, comprehension, path, and intervention. SUS measures perceived usability at a system level; it does not diagnose individual UI problems.

## Analysis
Separate observation from interpretation. Cluster evidence, examine contradictions, assess severity and task impact, and attach confidence. Frequency alone does not determine priority. A single safety or money-loss failure may be critical.

## Ethics
Get informed consent, disclose recording, minimize data, redact PII, secure files, define retention/deletion, allow withdrawal, and avoid collecting sensitive financial information unless essential and protected.

## Reporting
Provide decision, method, participants, limitations, task results, evidence-backed findings, what worked, unresolved questions, recommendations, and retest criteria. Label personas without direct evidence as proto-personas.

## Supplemental Guidance (Context-based Options)
From legacy systems: Any specific numerical targets for research (e.g., "always survey 20 users" or "5 interviews minimum") should be treated as context-based options. Actual sample sizes should be determined by statistical need and saturation, not rigidly enforced.
